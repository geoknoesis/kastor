Not another triple-store wrapper

RDF that fits how Kotlin apps are built

Kastor is built around a simple idea: your domain stays Kotlin-first, and RDF is always reachable through an explicit side channel—handles, generated vocabs, and a readable graph DSL—without dragging store-specific types through your business layer. On top of that, Kastor invests where few Kotlin stacks do: RDF 1.2 tracked against W3C syntax suites, a native SHACL 1.2 validator, Kastor Gen build-time codegen from shapes and vocabs, and ontology-quality tooling for governed vocabularies—not just a thin DSL over someone else’s parser. Swap Jena, RDF4J, in-memory graphs, or SPARQL endpoints with configuration; keep validation and reasoning visible, not magic.

What makes Kastor different

Most RDF stacks ask you to live inside Model, Statement, or vendor graph APIs from day one. Kastor meets you where Kotlin teams already work—clean domain boundaries—then connects to semantic infrastructure when you need it.

Goal Kastor Typical RDF APIs alone
Domain code without RDF types in the core Designed for it—pure Kotlin interfaces; RDF via handles & codegen Often couples business logic to graph APIs
Predictable bridge to triples Explicit side-channel access—no hidden mapping framework Direct models everywhere; ORMs vary
Vocabulary & shapes drive the API Vocabulary-agnostic DSL + Kastor Gen (SHACL / JSON-LD → Kotlin) Manual boilerplate or one-off mappings
Swap engines without rewriting the app Same patterns over Memory, Jena, RDF4J, SPARQL Usually locked to one stack’s surface area
RDF 1.2 on the standards track Providers exercised against W3C RDF 1.2 syntax suites (Turtle, TriG, N-Triples, N-Quads)—see RDF 1.2 conformance Often “close enough” without a published corpus matrix for your stack
SHACL 1.2 validation in-ecosystem Native engine with full SHACL 1.2 support (Core plus SPARQL extensions)—see SHACL validation Depends on adapters; rarely one cohesive Kotlin-first story
Codegen + quality as product surfaces Kastor Gen (Gradle/KSP) plus onto-quality catalogues and guides—see Kastor Gen and ontology quality Usually glued together from unrelated OSS bits

Kastor is not a triple database or SPARQL processor—it orchestrates Kotlin ergonomics on top of providers that are. See architecture and the docs hub for scope and tradeoffs.

Why teams adopt it

RDF 1.2 & SHACL 1.2 you can verify

RDF 1.2 syntax conformance is driven by the official W3C corpus for both Jena and RDF4J-backed paths; validation is covered by a native SHACL 1.2 engine (Core + SPARQL extensions), not an afterthought adapter. Details: RDF 1.2 conformance, SHACL.

Kastor Gen — ontologies at compile time

First-class KSP + Gradle codegen turns SHACL, JSON-LD, and vocabulary artifacts into Kotlin you actually maintain—interfaces, wrappers, and explicit ValidationContext hooks—not hand-written URI constants forever. Start at Kastor Gen.

Ontology quality, not only “valid RDF”

onto-quality and companion docs focus on OWL, SKOS, and shape hygiene for real catalogues and governed datasets—quality as a workflow, not a one-off linter. See ontology quality.

Compatibility layer, expressive surface

Keep TDB2, NativeStore, or SPARQL endpoints—same Kotlin graph DSL and SPARQL builders across providers; peel back to Jena or RDF4J when you must. Readable graphs without marrying business logic to vendor graph types.
